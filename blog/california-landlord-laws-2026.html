California Landlord Laws 2026: What Every Rental Property Owner Must Know

California added seven new compliance obligations for residential landlords in 2026. If you own rental property in the state and haven't reviewed your leases, appliances, and deposit procedures recently, at least one of these laws likely affects you right now.

Important disclaimer This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Laws vary by jurisdiction and change frequently. Always verify information with official sources and consult a licensed California attorney for advice specific to your situation.

1. AB 628 — Required Appliances in Rental Units

⚠️ Action required

Affects all California landlords with leases entered into, amended, or extended on or after January 1, 2026.

Assembly Bill 628 expands California's definition of a "habitable" rental unit. Starting January 1, 2026, landlords must provide and maintain a working stove and refrigerator in all residential rental units — with limited exceptions.

Previously, it was acceptable for tenants to supply their own refrigerator. That arrangement is now largely eliminated for new or renewed leases.

What's required

  • Every unit must have a working stove/oven and a working refrigerator
  • Recalled appliances must be repaired or replaced within 30 days of the recall notice
  • Appliances must be maintained in safe working order for the duration of the tenancy

Exceptions

AB 628 does not apply to: permanent supportive housing, single-room occupancy units with shared food preparation facilities, residential hotels, and units within facilities with communal kitchens such as assisted living.

Sources: Apartment Association of Orange County (AAOC), California Legislature, Property Management Pleasanton

What to do

Inspect every unit. If a tenant is currently providing their own refrigerator or stove, you are now required to supply one when the lease is next entered into, amended, or extended. Update your lease templates to reflect this obligation.

2. AB 414 — Modernized Security Deposit Returns

Review needed

Affects how you return security deposits and provide itemizations to tenants.

Assembly Bill 414 updates how landlords handle security deposits when a tenant moves out. The key change is that landlords and tenants can now agree to return deposits and provide itemized deductions electronically — including by email.

Key changes

  • Security deposit returns can now be made electronically with tenant consent
  • Itemized statements of deductions can be delivered by email after the tenant moves out
  • Existing deadlines for returning deposits remain unchanged — 21 days in California

Separately, under AB 12 (effective July 1, 2024), security deposits are already capped at one month's rent for most landlords. If your lease still references a two-month deposit for an unfurnished unit and you own more than two properties, that clause may be unenforceable.

Sources: Berkeley Rent Board, AAOC, Lifetime Property Management

What to do

Update your move-out procedures to allow electronic deposit returns if a tenant prefers it. Verify your lease reflects the current one-month deposit cap under AB 12 if you own more than two properties.

3. AB 1414 — Tenant Opt-Out for Bundled Services

Review needed if you bundle services into rent

Affects landlords who include internet, streaming, or other subscription services in rent.

Assembly Bill 1414 requires that tenants be given the option to opt out of any third-party subscription services bundled into their rent — such as internet, cable, or wireless services.

What this means

  • Applies to month-to-month tenancies and leases renewed on or after January 1, 2026
  • Tenants must be given a written opt-out option for bundled subscription services
  • If a landlord fails to provide the opt-out, tenants may deduct those charges from rent

Sources: AAOC, Property Management Pleasanton, California Legislature

What to do

If you bundle any subscription-based services into rent, add a written opt-out clause to your lease immediately. Do not continue charging for bundled services without giving tenants a documented way to decline them.

4. AB 747 — Fee Transparency in Advertising

Review needed

Affects how you advertise rental units online and in print.

Assembly Bill 747, known as the SPARE Act, requires landlords to disclose all mandatory fees upfront in any rental advertisement. If you advertise a unit at a certain price and add mandatory fees at the application stage, that's now prohibited.

What's required

  • All mandatory fees must be disclosed in any rental listing or advertisement
  • The advertised price must reflect the true total monthly cost the tenant will pay
  • Fees that are optional (like parking or storage) can still be listed separately

Sources: Lifetime Property Management, AAOC

What to do

Review all your rental listings on Zillow, Craigslist, or wherever you advertise. Make sure any mandatory fees are included in the advertised rent or clearly disclosed in the listing itself.

5. SB 610 — Disaster Protections for Tenants

FYI — Know this if you own in wildfire-prone areas

Particularly relevant for landlords in California's wildfire zones.

Senate Bill 610 expands landlord responsibilities when a natural or manmade disaster affects a rental property. If a property is impacted, landlords must take specific steps to protect tenants and may be required to halt or refund rent.

Key obligations

  • Landlords must remove debris and mitigate hazards such as mold or smoke damage following a disaster
  • Landlords must follow government cleanup protocols
  • Rent may need to be halted or refunded if the unit is uninhabitable due to disaster damage

Sources: Property Management Pleasanton, California Legislature

6. AB 1482 — Rent Cap Update for 2025–2026

⚠️ Critical — Most common source of landlord violations

If you're planning a rent increase, read this carefully before calculating the amount.

California's Tenant Protection Act (AB 1482) remains in full effect. It limits annual rent increases for covered properties to 5% plus the local Consumer Price Index (CPI), with a hard maximum of 10%.

For the current period (August 1, 2025 – July 31, 2026), the confirmed CPI for the San Francisco Bay Area is 1.3%, making the maximum allowable increase 6.3% for most covered Bay Area properties. Other regions have different CPI figures — always check your specific county.

Example calculation

Tenant pays $2,400/month in San Francisco. Maximum increase = $2,400 × 6.3% = $151.20. Maximum new rent = $2,551.20/month. You cannot go above this. You can only raise rent once every 12 months.

Does AB 1482 apply to your property?

AB 1482 covers most California residential rental properties in buildings older than 15 years. As of 2026, this means buildings with certificates of occupancy issued before 2011.

Generally exempt: Single-family homes and condos owned by a natural person (not a corporation or LLC) where the landlord has provided the required written notice of exemption. New construction under 15 years old. Owner-occupied duplexes.

Important: Local ordinances in cities like Los Angeles, San Francisco, Oakland, and Berkeley may impose even stricter limits. Always check your local ordinance in addition to AB 1482.

Just cause eviction requirements

AB 1482 also requires just cause to evict any tenant who has occupied a unit for 12 months or more. No-fault evictions require one month's rent in relocation assistance.

AB 1482 is set to expire January 1, 2030, unless extended by the California Legislature.

Sources: California Legislature (official bill text), Berkeley Rent Board, California Apartment Association, Southern California Rental Housing Association

What to do

Before issuing any rent increase notice: (1) confirm whether AB 1482 covers your property, (2) look up the current CPI for your specific county, (3) calculate the maximum allowable increase, (4) give proper written notice — 30 days for increases of 10% or less, 90 days for anything higher.

Quick Compliance Checklist for 2026

  • ✅ Every unit has a working stove and refrigerator (AB 628)
  • ✅ Lease reflects current one-month deposit cap for most landlords (AB 12)
  • ✅ Move-out procedures updated to allow electronic deposit returns (AB 414)
  • ✅ Bundled service opt-out clause added to leases if applicable (AB 1414)
  • ✅ All rental listings disclose mandatory fees upfront (AB 747)
  • ✅ Rent increase calculated using current CPI for your county (AB 1482)
  • ✅ AB 1482 exemption notice provided in writing if claiming exemption
  • ✅ Local ordinances checked — city rules may be stricter than state law
