Arizona Landlord Laws: What Every Rental Property Owner Must Know

Arizona's residential landlord-tenant law is governed by the Arizona Residential Landlord and Tenant Act, found in Title 33, Chapter 10 of the Arizona Revised Statutes. The state has specific rules on security deposits, repairs, landlord entry, and eviction — and 2025 brought a notable update for landlords who allow assistance animals.

Security Deposits

ℹ️ Arizona Revised Statutes § 33-1321

Arizona caps security deposits at one and one-half month's rent.

Under § 33-1321, a landlord may not demand or receive security — however denominated, including prepaid rent — in an amount or value of more than one and one-half month's rent. This cap does not prohibit a tenant from voluntarily paying more than that amount in advance.

Nonrefundable fees

The purpose of all nonrefundable fees or deposits must be stated in writing by the landlord. Any fee or deposit not designated as nonrefundable is refundable.

Move-in documentation

On move-in, a landlord must furnish the tenant with a signed copy of the lease, a move-in form for specifying any existing damages to the dwelling unit, and written notification that the tenant may be present at the move-out inspection. On request by the tenant, the landlord must notify the tenant of when the move-out inspection will occur.

Return timeline

Under § 33-1321, a landlord must return the security deposit — along with an itemized list of all deductions — within fourteen days, excluding Saturdays, Sundays or other legal holidays, after termination of the tenancy, delivery of possession, and demand by the tenant.

Permissible deductions

A landlord may deduct from the deposit all unpaid rent and, subject to the landlord's duty to mitigate, all charges as specified in the signed lease agreement or as provided in this chapter, as well as the amount of damages the landlord has suffered by reason of the tenant's noncompliance with § 33-1341 (tenant obligations).

Penalty for wrongful withholding

Under § 33-1321, if a landlord fails to comply with the return and itemization requirements, the tenant may recover the property and money owed, plus damages equal to twice the amount wrongfully withheld.

Repairs and Habitability

ℹ️ Arizona Revised Statutes § 33-1324

Landlords must keep premises fit, habitable, and in good working order.

Under § 33-1324, a landlord must:

  • Comply with the requirements of applicable building codes materially affecting health and safety
  • Make all repairs and do whatever is necessary to put and keep the premises in a fit and habitable condition
  • Keep all common areas of the premises in a clean and safe condition
  • Maintain in good and safe working order and condition all electrical, plumbing, sanitary, heating, ventilating, air-conditioning, and other facilities and appliances, including elevators, supplied or required to be supplied by the landlord
  • Provide and maintain appropriate receptacles and conveniences for the removal of ashes, garbage, rubbish, and other waste incidental to the occupancy of the dwelling unit, and arrange for their removal
  • Supply running water and reasonable amounts of hot water at all times, and reasonable heat and reasonable air-conditioning or cooling where such units are installed and offered, when required by seasonal weather conditions — except where the building that includes the dwelling unit is not required by law to be equipped for that purpose, or the dwelling unit is so constructed that heat, air-conditioning, cooling, or hot water is generated by an installation within the exclusive control of the tenant and supplied by a direct public utility connection

Tenant remedies for failure to repair

Under § 33-1361, if there is a material noncompliance by the landlord with the rental agreement, the tenant may deliver written notice specifying the breach; the rental agreement may be terminated if the landlord fails to remedy within ten days of receipt. If there is a noncompliance by the landlord with § 33-1324 that materially affects health and safety, the cure period is five days after receipt of notice.

In addition to termination, the tenant may recover damages and obtain injunctive relief for any noncompliance by the landlord with the rental agreement or with §§ 33-1318 or 33-1324.

Self-help repair

Under § 33-1363, if the reasonable cost of compliance is less than $300 or one-half of one month's rent — whichever amount is greater — the tenant may notify the landlord in writing. If the landlord fails to make the repair within ten days (or as promptly as conditions require in an emergency), the tenant may hire a licensed contractor, and after submitting an itemized statement and a waiver of lien to the landlord, deduct the actual and reasonable cost from rent. A tenant may not use this remedy for conditions caused by the deliberate or negligent act or omission of the tenant, a member of the tenant's family, or any other person on the premises with the tenant's consent.

Failure to supply essential services

Under § 33-1364, if contrary to the rental agreement or § 33-1324, a landlord deliberately or negligently fails to supply running water, gas or electrical service (or both if applicable), and reasonable amounts of hot water or heat, air-conditioning or cooling where such units are installed and offered, or essential services, the tenant may — after giving the landlord reasonable notice specifying the breach — procure those services and deduct their actual reasonable cost from rent, recover damages based on the diminution in the fair rental value of the dwelling unit, or procure reasonable substitute housing during the period of noncompliance (in which case rent is excused for that period).

Landlord Entry

ℹ️ Arizona Revised Statutes § 33-1343

Arizona requires at least two days' notice before most landlord entry.

Under § 33-1343, except in cases of emergency or when it is impracticable to do so, a landlord must give the tenant at least two days' notice before entering, and may enter only at reasonable times. Permitted reasons for entry include inspecting the premises, making necessary or agreed repairs, decorations, alterations, or improvements, supplying necessary or agreed services, and exhibiting the unit to prospective or actual purchasers, mortgagees, tenants, workmen, or contractors.

A landlord may enter without consent in case of emergency. The statute also prohibits a landlord from abusing the right to access or using it to harass the tenant.

If a tenant submits a written service or maintenance request to the landlord as prescribed in § 33-1341, that notice from the tenant constitutes permission for the landlord to enter for the sole purpose of acting on that request — no separate access notice from the landlord is required.

Lease Termination

ℹ️ Arizona Revised Statutes §§ 33-1318, 33-1375

Termination rules vary by tenancy type and circumstances.

Month-to-month tenancies

Under § 33-1375, either the landlord or the tenant may terminate a month-to-month tenancy by providing written notice at least thirty days prior to the periodic rental date specified in the notice.

Domestic violence and sexual assault

Under § 33-1318, a tenant who is a victim of domestic violence (as defined in § 13-3601), or who was the victim, in the tenant's dwelling, of sexual assault, may terminate a rental agreement early by providing written notice to the landlord requesting release from the rental agreement, with a mutually agreed-on release date within the next thirty days. The written notice must be accompanied by either a copy of a protective order issued under § 13-3602, or a copy of a written departmental report from a law enforcement agency stating that the tenant notified the law enforcement agency that the tenant was a victim of domestic violence or sexual assault.

The early termination right applies only if the actions, events, or circumstances that resulted in the tenant's status as a victim occurred within the thirty-day period immediately preceding the written notice to the landlord, unless this timeframe is waived by the landlord. A tenant who meets these requirements avoids liability for future rent and may not be charged early termination penalties or fees.

Eviction Process

ℹ️ Arizona Revised Statutes § 33-1368

Arizona evictions follow specific notice requirements before a landlord may file in court.

Under § 33-1368, if rent is unpaid when due and the tenant fails to pay within five days after receiving written notice of nonpayment and the landlord's intention to terminate if rent is not paid, the landlord may terminate the rental agreement by filing a special detainer action.

Reinstatement before filing

Before a special detainer action is filed, the rental agreement is reinstated if the tenant pays all past due and unpaid periodic rent plus a reasonable late fee set forth in a written rental agreement.

Reinstatement after filing

After a special detainer action is filed, reinstatement requires the tenant to pay all past due rent, reasonable late fees set forth in a written rental agreement, attorney fees, and court costs.

Lease violations

For non-payment-related lease violations, the notice period varies. For a material breach of the rental agreement, the landlord must provide written notice specifying the breach and allowing at least ten days to cure before the agreement may be terminated. For noncompliance with § 33-1341 that materially affects health and safety, the cure period is at least five days.

Irreparable breaches

Certain conduct allows the landlord to terminate without providing a cure period. The statute lists: the illegal discharge of a weapon, homicide, prostitution, criminal street gang activity, activity prohibited under § 13-2308, unlawful manufacturing, selling, transferring, possessing, using, or storing of a controlled substance, threatening or intimidating, assault, acts found to constitute a nuisance under § 12-991, and any breach that otherwise jeopardizes the health, safety, and welfare of the landlord, the landlord's agent, or another tenant, or that involves imminent or actual serious property damage. The statute explicitly states this list is not exhaustive.

HB 2068 — Assistance Animal Liability (2025)

ℹ️ Signed May 13, 2025

Creates new definitions and a landlord liability shield for assistance animals.

House Bill 2068, signed by the Governor on May 13, 2025, added definitions of "assistance animal" and "service animal" to Arizona's fair housing statutes and created a landlord liability protection. Under the new provision, a landlord is not liable for injuries or damages caused by a purported assistance animal or purported service animal that is allowed on the property as a reasonable accommodation — while tenant disability rights are preserved.

Key Takeaways

  • ✅ Security deposits are capped at one and one-half month's rent and must be returned within 14 business days of tenancy termination, delivery of possession, and demand by the tenant; wrongful withholding carries a penalty of twice the amount wrongfully withheld
  • ✅ Landlords must keep premises habitable, maintain all systems in working order, and supply running water, hot water, and reasonable heat and cooling; tenants may pursue repair-and-deduct for qualifying repairs up to $300 or half the monthly rent, whichever is greater
  • ✅ Landlords must give at least two days' notice before entering — except in emergencies; tenant maintenance requests constitute permission for entry without a separate notice
  • ✅ Month-to-month tenancies require 30 days' written notice from either party to terminate
  • ✅ Domestic violence and sexual assault victims may terminate early with 30 days' notice, documentation, and proof the incident occurred within the preceding 30 days
  • ✅ Nonpayment of rent requires a 5-day written notice before a landlord may file a special detainer action; material lease violations require at least 10 days to cure (5 days for health and safety violations)
  • ✅ HB 2068 (signed May 13, 2025) created a landlord liability shield for injuries caused by assistance animals allowed as reasonable accommodations
